# Rebalancing

## Overview

When you start investing, you typically choose an asset allocation that fits your risk tolerance.

For example, a classic beginner portfolio might consist of **80% Stocks** (for growth) and **20% Bonds** (for stability).

Over time, market movements will change these proportions. Stocks might surge, or bonds might outperform, causing your portfolio to drift away from your target.

**Rebalancing** is the process of bringing your portfolio back to your original target allocation.

## Why Rebalance?

1. **Risk Management:** If stocks grow from 80% to 95% of your portfolio, your investment becomes riskier than you originally intended. A market crash affects you more.
2. **Buy Low, Sell High:** Rebalancing forces you to systematically sell some of your high-performing, expensive assets and buy more of your underperforming, cheaper assets.

## The Basic Math

Rebalancing math only requires four simple steps:

1. **Calculate Total Portfolio Value:** Add up the current value of all your investments.
2. **Calculate Target Values:** Multiply the total portfolio value by your original target percentages.
3. **Compare Current vs. Target:** Subtract the current value of each asset from its target value.
4. **Execute Trades:**
    - If the result is **negative**, you have too much of that asset and need to **sell** the difference.
    - If the result is **positive**, you have too little and need to **buy** the difference.

## Examples

### Stock/Bonds

Let's walk through an example with a **80/20 Stock/Bond portfolio** that started with $100,000.

#### 1. Starting Point

* **Stocks (80%):** $80,000
* **Bonds (20%):** $20,000

#### 2. The Market Shifts

During the year, the stock market had a massive bull run and jumped by 30%. The bond market was sluggish and dropped by 5%.

Your new **Current Values** are:

* **Stocks:** `$80,000 x 1.30 = $104,000`
* **Bonds:**  `$20,000 x 0.95 = $19,000`

#### 3. Rebalance

- **Step 1: Total Portfolio Value**:
    - `$104,000 + $19,000 = $123,000`
- **Step 2: Calculate Target Values:**
    - **Stocks (80%):** `$123,000 x .80 = $98,400`
    - **Bonds  (20%):** `$123,000 x .20 = $24,600`
- **Step 3: Determine Adjustments Needed:**
    - **Stocks:**: `$98,000 (target) - $104,000 (current) = -$5,600 (negative = sell)`
    - **Bonds :**  `$24,600 (target) - $19,000 (current)  = +5,600 (positive = buy)`
- **Step 4: . The Action Plan**
    - **Sell $5,600** worth of your stocks.
    - **Buy $5,600** worth of bonds with those proceeds.

After doing this, your portfolio is perfectly reset to its 80/20 split, locking in some stock profits and buying bonds while they are cheaper.

## Stocks (US/International) / Bonds

Things get a little more complicated when you have a sub-asset class such as US/International Stocks.

To make this easy, simplify the portfolio into three parts.

For example, if you want to maintain an 80/20 stocks/bond allocation, but a 70/30 us/international stock ratio:

- Stock `(80%)`
    - US = `.80 stocks * .70 us = 56% `
    - International = `.80 stocks x .30 int = 24%`
- Bonds `(20%)`

So now we apply the Stocks/Bonds rebalancing steps above but with 3 asset classes:

- `56%` US Stocks
- `24%` International Stocks
- `20%` Bonds


## When Should You Rebalance?

### Calendar-Based

In tax-deferred (401k, 401a, 403b, 457b, IRA, etc.) and tax-free (Roth-IRA, HSA) accounts, an annual rebalancing
should serve the average investor well. Pick a memorable date such as a birthday or anniversary and execute your
rebalancing every year near/on this date.

### Threshold-Based

If you are rebalancing in taxable accounts you have to be wary of incurring additional taxes when selling assets. There are different strategies such as using threshold bands (e.g. allocation more than 20% drift, etc.) but these
are beyond our discussion here.
